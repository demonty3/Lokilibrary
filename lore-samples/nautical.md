# The Harbour Year

The harbour never sleeps. Before the tide turns, every vessel at the port makes
ready to sail. The mariners read the wave and the wind, and the lighthouse throws
its beacon across the water. Out past the shore, the sea runs deep and the coast
falls away.

Old Doran has fished this sea for forty seasons. His vessel knows the harbour
mouth by the set of the tide, and no mariner reads the coast better. At dawn the
fishing fleet leaves port together; by dusk the harbour fills again, sail after
sail, the lighthouse beacon guiding each vessel home along the shore.

A coast is its harbour. A vessel is its mariner. The tide keeps the only clock
the port obeys; the wave writes the only law the sea will sign. Walk the shore
and you can count the trade in masts: a deep-water vessel at the far quay, a
fishing boat at the near, the lighthouse over all.

When the tide is full the whole port turns out. Every vessel rides the harbour
swell, every mariner watches the wave, and the lighthouse beacon sweeps the coast
from shore to open sea.

Sea, ocean, ship, harbour, tide, sail, wave, coast, shore, port, mariner, vessel,
lighthouse, fishing, anchor, beacon — the vocabulary of a life lived by the water.
