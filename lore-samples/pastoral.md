# The Settled Valley

The village wakes early. In every field the harvest is near, and the orchard
hangs heavy with fruit. Farm hands cross the meadow at dawn, baskets ready for
the harvest. The pasture rolls green beyond the orchard, and each garden along
the lane is in full bloom.

Old Maren keeps the largest farm in the village. Her orchard is known three
valleys over; her meadow feeds more sheep than any pasture in the district. When
the harvest comes, the whole village walks the field together, and the garden
gates stand open from the first bloom to the last.

A village is its fields. A farm is its harvest. The meadow remembers every
season, the orchard every pruning, the pasture every flock. Walk the garden
paths and you can read the year in the bloom: early bloom in the kitchen garden,
late bloom in the far field, the orchard heavy between.

By harvest's end the village gathers at the meadow's edge. The farm carts roll in
from every field and pasture. The orchard gives its last fruit, the garden its
last bloom, and the village counts the harvest as it has for a hundred years.

Field, farm, meadow, orchard, pasture, garden, village, harvest, bloom — the
plain words of a settled life on the land.
